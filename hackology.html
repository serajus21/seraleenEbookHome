Hacking into a communications system is nearly as old as communication systems themselves

Napoleon's semaphore telegraph, which used mechanical arms on towers that looked a bit like windmills to send coded signals across France, was hacked in 1834 by thieves seeking stock market information. Intercepted signals played a role in the American civil war: the Union and Confederate armies tapped each other's telegraph lines, and Confederate newspapers even published coded Union messages to try to crowdsource code-breaking.

That interplay of offence and defence, invention and counter-invention, courses through the many meanings of the word hacking. The term dates back to 1955, when graduate students from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) used it to mean, roughly, any creative trial-and-error experiments with a machine. Today, criminal hackers mount cyberattacks to enable identity theft, blackmail victims, or steal money with a click of a mouse.

Yet "white-hat" hackers—or security experts hired to test IT weaknesses—are playing a bigger role in cybersecurity, finding and fixing vulnerabilities before the criminals spot them. "Hacking is trying to break things apart, put them back together, and creatively find solutions around limitations," says Rachel Tobac, CEO of SocialProof Security. "White-hat" hackers like her, says Ms Tobac, aim to “understand where the holes and gaps may be, so we can fix them and patch them."

Often, the humans involved in a system, not the mechanisms, are the weak link. Modern offensive hacking started with the "phone phreaks" of the 1960s and 1970s, who gamed the US telephone system. Some discovered that they could make free long-distance phone calls by blowing a whistle into a phone at the high-pitched frequency of 2,600 hertz, to mimic an AT&T switching signal. Others befriended phone company operatives to learn their professional terminology, then used test numbers or operator assists, as a technician would, to avoid phone charges.

In the 1980s hackers became infamous for breaking into computers via modems. The 1983 film WarGames, starring Matthew Broderick as a teenage hacker who nearly starts World War III, dramatised hacking, inspired it and fuelled a backlash. "War dialing" became the term for auto-dialing numbers to look for modems at government agencies, companies and bulletin board systems. The film even helped to inspire the US Congress to enact anti-hacking laws such as the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act of 1986. "Things [hackers] were doing to test the limitations of the system became something you could be jailed for," says Ms Tobac.
